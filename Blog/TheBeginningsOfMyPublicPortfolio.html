The Beginnings of my Public Portfolio

Web development, August 26th 2020

It feels like it’s been a while since my last update, and since then I’ve introduced a lot of new features. I’ve had some time to reflect on what I’ve completed thus far, and what needed to be added.

But first, I want to take a moment to introduce another project I completed in this time. I stated in the last blog that I’d like to create something with utility. I’ve done just that.

I have designed and implemented a directory of pages that have replaced the ‘new tab’ page that browsers present when opening a new tab/window. I was tired of this page being both dull and impersonal – it provided nothing for me.

Instead, I now have a functional layout that directs me to different categories important for my productivity, and then provides catalogues of useful sites related to the category. I have of course made this responsive and it is my default page on my phone as well. It speeds up my processes, from programming to online shopping, as all the links I may need are always immediately available.

These pages remain hidden from my site though, meaning I am more excited to present the new feature public to all – a portfolio.

I have been dabbling in the creation of little apps and programs for a short while, and decided they are best shared with everyone. I share everything on my blog, so why not have these programs free for everyone to use? Don’t get me wrong, at this point in time these apps are far from astounding, but they provide a timeline of my progress through different programming languages.

Most importantly, this feature is expanding my knowledge of JavaScript. I have implemented little ‘show more’ buttons to keep my portfolio and blog homepages concise whilst flexing that little bit of extra JavaScript functionality.

The apps themselves thus far focus around the use of JavaScript – a clock, counter and calculator for practising the use of calculation functions and a cocktail manual for the hiding and displaying of elements. Sure, they are simple, but they have functionality and that’s what makes me proud. If anything, they are a yardstick that reminds me how far I have come and act as motivation to push on.

The plan is for future projects to incorporate PHP and server-side functionality – but I will not rush and risk confusing myself. Learning web development is a marathon, not a sprint.
